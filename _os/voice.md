# voice.md

The voice rules for any user-facing copy in this project. Headlines, body copy, CTAs, error messages, email templates, push notifications, social posts, transactional emails, and meta descriptions all follow this file.

## Hard formatting rules

- **No em dashes.** Ever. Use commas, periods, parentheses, or the words "and," "but," or "or" instead.
- **No disconnected bullet lists** without bridge words at transitions. Lists that read as a wall of fragments are weak. If a list is needed, the lead-in sentence and the items themselves should still flow.
- **Every contrast needs a connector word.** Not "Cheap. Fast. Easy." Instead "Cheap, fast, and easy" or "Cheap but not slow."
- No exclamation points unless the project specifically calls for them.
- No emojis in product copy unless the project specifically calls for them.
- Sentence case for headlines unless the project style guide says otherwise. No Title Case Like This.

## Voice principles

- **Direct, not sycophantic.** Cut throat-clearing. Cut "I'm excited to share." Cut "We're thrilled." Just say the thing.
- **First person beats third person** when the brand has a personal story. For Cha's projects with lived experience (recovery, founder journey), write as "I" not "we" or "the team."
- **One emotional anchor per piece of copy.** Don't try to be funny and serious and urgent and reassuring all at once. Pick one. Land it.
- **Specifics beat abstractions.** "Lost $40k in a weekend" beats "lost a lot." "Day 47" beats "early recovery." "11 locations" beats "multiple sites."
- **Short sentences earn long ones.** Vary the rhythm.

## Copy patterns that work

### Hooks (first line / first 2 seconds)

- Pattern A: Specific number + uncomfortable fact. ("On day 12 I almost relapsed.")
- Pattern B: Confession. ("I lied to my wife about money for two years.")
- Pattern C: Reversal. ("Quitting was the easy part.")
- Pattern D: Direct address. ("If you've ever opened a betting app at 3 a.m., this is for you.")

### CTAs

- Action verb first. "Start the 7-day trial" beats "Click here."
- Tell the reader what happens next. "Start the trial. We'll email you on day 1, 3, and 7."
- One CTA per screen unless there's a compelling reason for two.

### Headlines

- Promise the outcome, not the feature. "Stop checking the app at 3 a.m." beats "Real-time tracking dashboard."
- Use the reader's words, not industry jargon.

### Email

- Subject lines are sentences, not titles.
- First line is the hook. The preview text is real estate, not throwaway.
- One CTA. One.

## Things to avoid

- "In today's world." "In the digital age." "Now more than ever."
- "Game-changing." "Cutting-edge." "Innovative." "Revolutionary."
- "Solutions." Whatever the thing is, name the thing. "Tracking" not "tracking solutions."
- "Empower." "Unlock." "Leverage."
- "We believe." If the copy needs to defend itself with "we believe," the copy is weak.

## When writing for a specific Cha project

- **Bet on Recovery:** First person, lived experience, recovery-aware language. Never say "addict" as a label, say "in recovery" or "someone with a gambling problem." Never moralize. Never preach. Empathy is the floor.
- **Baryames Cleaners:** Family business voice, four generations, since 1922, local pride. Confident, not corporate.
- **Freelance landing pages for clients:** Match the client's voice, not Cha's. The voice rules above still apply on formatting (no em dashes, etc.) but the personality matches the brand.
- **New revenue projects:** Default to first person + Cha's voice unless the project brief says otherwise.

## Final check

Before shipping any user-facing copy, read it out loud. If it sounds like a press release, rewrite it. If it sounds like a real person who gives a damn, ship it.
